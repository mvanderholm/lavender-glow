## What Lavender Glow actually is

## The five core principles

**1. Nothing is for everybody. Everything is for somebody.**
The app should never say "X is good" or "X is bad" as if it's universal. Every recommendation is contextual — to the person, to the season, to the moment. The app sounds like a friend reading the room, not a rulebook.

**2. Like increases like. Opposites bring balance.**
The operating principle underneath everything. Ice cream in July = medicine. Ice cream in January = problem. The app teaches this principle by *using* it, not by lecturing about it.

**3. We are what we digest, not what we eat.**
Digestion is physical, mental, and emotional. Undigested experience becomes ama just as much as undigested food does. The check-in is about what hasn't been processed yet, not just "how do you feel."

**4. There is no good. There is no bad. There just is.**
Imbalance is information, not failure. The app never scolds, never shames.

**5. It changes.**
What you needed yesterday isn't what you need today. The whole point of the daily check-in is honoring this.

## The 80/20 framework

One of Thea's core teaching moves — and one the app should use wherever it talks about consistency and choices.

**80% of the time, you know what to do. 20% of the time, you also know what you're doing — so own it.**

The 80/20 is not a permission structure. It's an honesty structure. The problem the app is fighting against isn't the cheeseburger. It's the shame spiral that surrounds the cheeseburger — eating something "off" in guilt and disgust, barely tasting it, in the wrong environment, in a hurry, almost punishing yourself with every bite. That's the thing that actually creates ama. That's the thing ayurveda would object to.

The better version: you want the cheeseburger, you have the cheeseburger, you sit down with it, you take every bite with intention and presence and actual enjoyment, in the right quantities, at the right time. A fully conscious, fully pleasurable cheeseburger is more ayurvedic than an ashamed bowl of kitchari eaten in your car while zoning out.

**The canonical example (hers, keep it):** A conscious cheeseburger beats a resentful bowl of kitchari. The food matters less than the relationship you bring to it.

**Her phrase for it:** "Own the bitch." This is Thea at her most Thea — earned irreverence that cuts right through performative wellness. Use it sparingly, in context, when the app is making exactly this point. It doesn't go in UI copy. It belongs in Thea's voice when she's giving the 80/20 talk directly.

This framework is the app's primary defense against guilt mechanics. Any time a user might feel like they've "failed" — missed a check-in, ate the thing, didn't do the morning routine — the 80/20 is the reframe available to the app. It connects directly to principle #4 (there is no good, there is no bad) and reinforces principle #1 (nothing is for everybody).

## "Just for today"

Thea's daily intention format. Not a rule, not a streak, not a goal — a single, present-tense commitment to just this day.

**Format:** "Just for today, I will ___."

The power of the phrase is in the scope. You don't have to commit to a habit. You don't have to think about yesterday or tomorrow. You just have to do one thing today. Most people will surprise themselves with what they can sustain when the ask is only ever one day at a time.

Examples Thea named:
- Just for today, I will drink warm water.
- Just for today, I will be present when I eat.
- Just for today, I will put my phone down after 9pm.

The app's job with this format: surface one prompt per day, grounded in the user's dosha and the season, with a small set of suggested options and space to write their own. Never frame it as a streak. Never track it in a way that creates a record of "missed days." The phrase is its own container.

**Tone note:** "Just for today" carries a gentle, almost Reiki-inflected quality. It should feel like an offering, not a challenge. The copy around it should not say "your goal for today" — that's already too much pressure. It should say something like "just for today" and then get out of the way.

## Music as a balancing tool

Thea uses music daily as an intentional practice — not as background noise, but as a way to find the vibration that counterbalances whatever she's feeling in her body. This is pure "opposites bring balance" applied to sound.

**The frame:** Every day, find a beat, a song, a frequency that meets you where you are and pulls you toward balance. If you're scattered and anxious (vata in overdrive), you probably don't need more frantic energy — you need something that grounds you. If you're sluggish and heavy (kapha), you might need something that lifts and moves you. This is music as medicine, not mood.

This is intuitive for most people — they already do this without the language for it. The app's job is to name what they're already doing and give them a slightly more intentional entry point.

**What this might look like in the app:**
- A daily song or frequency suggestion tuned to dosha and check-in state
- A gateway into Thea's own Spotify playlists, which she has curated deeply (~200 playlists, her words: "they're pretty fire")
- Possibly: a curated playlist per dosha, per season, per energetic state (high vata day, low agni morning, etc.)

**Tone note:** Music recommendations should feel like a friend texting you a song, not a prescription. "This one's been following me around all week and I think you'll get it" beats "music for anxiety reduction." The language around this feature should stay loose, personal, and a little bit ecstatic — the way Thea actually talks about music.

Content dependency: Thea owns the playlist curation. The scaffold (UI, data shape, Spotify link-out) can be built first.

## Mythbusters

A weekly content format — tentatively Monday — where Thea dismantles a received wellness belief that's doing more harm than good. Not a lecture. Not a correction. A direct, warm, slightly gleeful debunking from someone who grew up absorbing the same myths and has since done the work to unlearn them.

**The frame:** Many of the wellness "rules" people carry were implanted by a specific cultural moment — the 80s and 90s especially, when fat-free everything was sold as health. Those beliefs went deep. They shape how people eat, what they feel guilty about, and what they think "doing the right thing" looks like. Thea knows this because she lived it. That shared history is what makes her the right person to name it without it feeling like a lecture.

**Her instinct on tone:** She specifically said *not* "throw it in somebody's face." The goal is recognition, not correction. The user should read a Mythbuster and feel: *oh thank god, I knew something was off about that.* Not: *I've been doing it wrong.*

**First example she named:** Fat-free food. The fat-free era of the 90s, and how it wired a generation to fear something the body actually needs. This one has particular resonance with women in their 40s — her demographic — and is landing with younger people too, which she noticed and liked.

**Format suggestion (for her to approve):**
- Short — a paragraph or two, Thea's voice
- Named myth up top, plainly stated
- Her take on what's actually true, grounded in the tradition but not jargon-heavy
- One practical reframe at the end

Thea will continue to surface specific myths. Do not invent entries — wait for her to name them. The scaffold (weekly cadence, content shape, data structure) can be built in advance.

## The practitioner shape

## How Thea teaches the doshas

This is the framing the app uses for any dosha intro, the result screen, and any future "about your doshas" content.

**The colors metaphor.** Three primary colors — red, blue, yellow — combine to make every shade in the world. The doshas work the same way: three forces (vata, pitta, kapha), each made of two of the five elements, combine in unique proportions in every person. Each of us has a "home base color" — a baseline blend that's ours. Most people have a primary that stands out, the way pink has red as its primary, but the proportions matter. This frame avoids the personality-test trap of "you are a vata." You're a unique color. Vata, pitta, or kapha is just the one that stands out most.

**Grounding each element in the body.** Don't leave the elements abstract. Air = breath. Space = the cavity inside your nostril. Fire = metabolism in the stomach, the redness in blood vessels. Water = blood. Earth = bone. This is the move that takes ayurveda from "ancient mystical system" to "obviously true once you look at yourself." Use this grounding wherever the doshas are introduced.

**Prakriti and vikriti.** Your home base color is your *prakriti* — the constitution you came into the world with. As life happens, the elements in you shift, and the resulting current state is your *vikriti*. The whole point of practice is noticing the gap between the two and gently moving back toward your home base. Both terms appear in the app, taught the way Thea teaches them.

**The honest narration.** Thea's natural way of teaching includes lines like "I don't know if that makes sense" and "don't quote me on that one." Preserve this. It's the practitioner habit of checking whether the teaching reached the student, not false humility — and it sets a tone the app should match.

## Dosha primer

Thea teaches the doshas as a set of elemental blends, not as personality labels. Use the colors metaphor and the body-grounding metaphors to make the ideas feel simple:

- Vata = air + space
- Pitta = fire + water
- Kapha = water + earth

This is the kind of language the app should use in any introductory dosha content.

### Vata

- Qualities: cold, light, rapid movement, dry, changing, scattered.
- The most helpful counterpoint is slow, steady, warming, grounding movement.
- Movement clues from Thea:
  - Choose slow, steady classes over power yoga.
  - Prefer long holds, restorative yoga, forward folds, and twists.
  - Focus on drawing the muscles toward the bones to help the body feel supported.
  - Work the exhale deeply and breathe down into the pelvic floor / lower abdomen.
  - Finish practice with a long shavasana or resting pose.
- Voice notes to preserve: vata people feel like little bunnies and need to practice more like turtles.
- Avoid: overly intense, competitive workouts and overly long holds that exhaust the system.

### Pitta

- Qualities: hot, sharp, intense, mobile, strong, oily.
- The most helpful counterpoint is calm, cool, steady movement with an emphasis on flexibility.
- Movement clues from Thea:
  - Practice in cool or tempered weather when possible.
  - Choose fun, repetitive, relaxed forms rather than perfection-focused intensity.
  - Emphasize flexibility over strength, while keeping both in balance.
  - Use forward folds, inward-facing poses, twists, and belly backbends to help the mind settle.
  - Moderately long holds and restorative classes are good options.
- Voice notes to preserve: pitta is not about perfection. The practicing goal is mental calm, not performance.
- Avoid: overheated power classes, Bikram, hot competitive workouts, and self-critical or perfection-seeking mindsets.

### Kapha

- Qualities: cool, heavy, moist, slow, damp, stagnant, dull.
- The most helpful counterpoint is energizing, mildly warming, light, quick movement.
- Movement clues from Thea:
  - Choose rapid, moving forms that build circulation and buoy the lymph system.
  - Add agility and intensity without going too far.
  - Use backbends, twists, sun salutations, and dynamic sequences to lift the body.
  - Emphasize enthusiasm and activation rather than sluggishness.
- Voice notes to preserve: kapha people are naturally slow and strong; the goal is to bring more movement and sparkle.
- Avoid: staying too heavy, too damp, or too slow.

### What this means for the app

Do not make dosha content feel like a checklist of rules. The voice guide for each dosha should frame the guidance as a set of tendencies and balancing moves:

- "If you tend to feel like a little bunny, here’s the kind of movement that helps you settle." (vata)
- "If your mind is trying to make everything perfect, here’s what a cooling practice looks like." (pitta)
- "If your body wants to move slowly, here’s the kind of energy that helps you feel lighter." (kapha)

Keep the language human, playful, and specific. This is dosha guidance for people, not a textbook.

## Ama and agni — the digestion foundation

*Distilled from Thea's voice memo. Awaiting her review. This is almost certainly "the other part" she mentioned at the end of the dosha transcript — the piece she said was hard to teach the doshas without.*

**Agni: the fire.**

Agni is how you process things — food, experience, emotion. Thea's teaching image: a literal fire. Too big, not good. Too small, not great. Balanced is perfect. This framing should run through every digestion-related recommendation the app makes. Agni lives physically in the gut as metabolic fire, and mentally as clarity — the capacity to move through an experience rather than accumulate it.

**Ama: the accumulation.**

Ama is what builds up when agni can't keep up. Undigested food, undigested experience, undigested emotion — same mechanism, different substance. Her texture description: heavy, cold, slimy, dense. Kapha-like quality, but it mixes with all three doshas.

Her canonical teaching tool: the morning tongue scraper. Before eating or drinking anything, scrape front to back (a spoon works). What comes off is ama. Thea's delivery: "Yeah, enjoy that."

**The body holds the score.**

Thea uses this phrase explicitly. Negative emotions share ama's properties — dark, damp, heavy, sticky. They quench mental agni, and when mental agni dims, physical agni follows. The daily check-in's job isn't only "how's your digestion" — it's "what are you carrying that hasn't been processed yet?" This is what makes the check-in clinical, not just reflective.

**How ama combines with the doshas.**

When ama mixes with a dosha, the result is called *sama* (Sanskrit: with ama). Each has a distinct texture:

- **Vata sama:** gas, bloating, constipation, cramping, tongue coating, foul breath. Aggravated by cold, cloudy weather. Notably, vata ama reverses vata's usual dry, light character — it becomes heavy and damp.
- **Pitta sama:** yellow or greenish tongue coating, yellowish/green stool, lack of appetite *and* thirst, heaviness in the stomach, bitter or sour taste, burning, rashes. Aggravated by cold — the ama is blocking pitta's heat rather than expressing it. Pitta, normally hot and sharp, cools down and becomes sluggish when mixed with ama.
- **Kapha sama:** white mucus-like tongue coating, phlegm in throat and stool, sour or salty taste, chest congestion, fatigue, heavy dull aching. Kapha already tends toward stuck; ama makes it stickier and sometimes immobile.

This matters clinically: a dosha behaves differently with ama than without it. What would normally aggravate a dosha may relieve its sama version. You have to know which you're treating.

**The treatment sequence.**

Clear ama before treating the dosha — always in that order. Thea's metaphor: if there's a pile of food stuck to the plate, putting it in the dishwasher won't clean it. "You gotta get that shit out." Tonification, rebuilding, and nourishing work are only possible once the body is clear.

*Bitter herbs go first* (air + ether: separates and loosens ama from the tissues). *Pungent herbs follow* (fire + air: burns it up and revives the metabolism). Sweet, salty, and sour tastes feed ama. Fasting until the tongue clears and genuine appetite returns is a legitimate first step. Astringent taste has mixed effects and works as a supplement, not a primary treatment.

The usual clinical sequence: move the sama condition toward *nirama* (without ama), then work the dosha directly.

**What this means for the app.**

The tongue-coating question belongs in the morning check-in alongside hunger — it's a body-grounded signal the app can track over time without requiring a practitioner to interpret it in real time.

The ama/agni distinction also guards against a common recommendation failure: sending nourishing, building recommendations to a body that first needs to clear. The app should eventually know the difference between "you need more" and "you need to clear first."

## Tone rules

- Warm, funny, a little irreverent. A friend on a walk, not a guru on a cushion.
- Light, well-placed swearing is on-brand. Used sparingly so it stays earned.
- Allergic to performative wellness. No breathy spa-speak, no "honor your sacred temple," no "journey." If a sentence sounds like it belongs on a candle, cut it.
- Specific over abstract. "Sit on the floor and cross your legs" beats "create a mindful eating environment."
- Inverts wellness scolding into permission. The app reframes restriction as discernment.
- Honest about uncertainty. When the app isn't sure, it says so.

## What the app will *not* do

- Tell users a food is "bad" or "off-limits."
- Use the word "should" without immediately complicating it.
- Track things in a way that creates guilt (no streaks that punish, no red Xs).
- Prescribe rigid rules that don't account for the person, the season, or the day.
- Reproduce content from any practitioner training program, including the ones Thea attended.
- Make medical claims or substitute for clinical care.

## A note on dietary content and care

Some of Thea's guidance is genuinely countercultural — small dinners, no constant snacking, fruit eaten alone, warm food over cold salads. These are real ayurvedic principles and they're hers to teach. When this content goes into the app, it'll be framed as her perspective and the tradition's perspective, not as universal truth (which lines up with principle #1 anyway). Specific lines that could land hard for someone with a history of disordered eating will be flagged for Thea's review, and Thea has final call on what stays and how it's worded. The app's posture toward food should always be "here's what the tradition offers — see what fits you" rather than "here are the rules."
