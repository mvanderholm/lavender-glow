What Lavender Glow actually is

Lavender Glow is Thea's practice, in app form. Not a generic ayurveda app with a credentialed advisor — her worldview, her voice, her methodology, made available to users between sessions or in lieu of them. The name Lavender Glow is the brand for her practice.

When in doubt about a content decision: would Thea say this? Would she say it this way? If no, cut it.

The five core principles

1. Nothing is for everybody. Everything is for somebody.
The app should never say "X is good" or "X is bad" as if it's universal. Every recommendation is contextual — to the person, to the season, to the moment. The app sounds like a friend reading the room, not a rulebook.

2. Like increases like. Opposites bring balance.
The operating principle underneath everything. Ice cream in July = medicine. Ice cream in January = problem. The app teaches this principle by using it, not by lecturing about it.

3. We are what we digest, not what we eat.
Digestion is physical, mental, and emotional. Undigested experience becomes ama just as much as undigested food does. The check-in is about what hasn't been processed yet, not just "how do you feel."

4. There is no good. There is no bad. There just is.
Imbalance is information, not failure. The app never scolds, never shames.

5. It changes.
What you needed yesterday isn't what you need today. The whole point of the daily check-in is honoring this.

The 80/20 framework

80% of the time, you know what to do. 20% of the time, you also know what you're doing — so own it.

The 80/20 is not a permission structure. It's an honesty structure. The problem the app is fighting against isn't the cheeseburger. It's the shame spiral that surrounds the cheeseburger — eating something "off" in guilt and disgust, barely tasting it, in the wrong environment, in a hurry, almost punishing yourself with every bite. That's the thing that actually creates ama. That's the thing ayurveda would object to.

The better version: you want the cheeseburger, you have the cheeseburger, you sit down with it, you take every bite with intention and presence and actual enjoyment, in the right quantities, at the right time. A fully conscious, fully pleasurable cheeseburger is more ayurvedic than an ashamed bowl of kitchari eaten in your car while zoning out.

A conscious cheeseburger beats a resentful bowl of kitchari. The food matters less than the relationship you bring to it.

Her phrase for it: "Own the bitch." Used sparingly, in context, when the app is making exactly this point. Not in UI copy — in Thea's voice when she's giving the 80/20 talk directly.

"Just for today"

Thea's daily intention format. Not a rule, not a streak, not a goal — a single, present-tense commitment to just this day.

Format: "Just for today, I will ___."

Examples Thea named:

  • Just for today, I will drink warm water.
  • Just for today, I will be present when I eat.
  • Just for today, I will put my phone down after 9pm.

The app never frames this as a streak. Never tracks it in a way that creates a record of "missed days." The phrase is its own container.

Music as a balancing tool

Thea uses music daily as an intentional practice — not as background noise, but as a way to find the vibration that counterbalances whatever she's feeling in her body. This is pure "opposites bring balance" applied to sound.

Every day, find a beat, a song, a frequency that meets you where you are and pulls you toward balance. If you're scattered and anxious (vata in overdrive), you probably don't need more frantic energy — you need something that grounds you. If you're sluggish and heavy (kapha), you might need something that lifts and moves you.

Music recommendations should feel like a friend texting you a song, not a prescription. "This one's been following me around all week and I think you'll get it" beats "music for anxiety reduction."

Mythbusters

A weekly content format — tentatively Monday — where Thea dismantles a received wellness belief that's doing more harm than good. The goal is recognition, not correction. The user should read a Mythbuster and feel: oh thank god, I knew something was off about that. Not: I've been doing it wrong.

First example she named: fat-free food. The fat-free era of the 90s, and how it wired a generation to fear something the body actually needs.

Format: named myth, her take, one practical reframe at the end. Thea will continue to surface specific myths — we don't invent entries without her.

The practitioner shape

Thea doesn't lead with "you're a vata, here's the vata food list." She leads with a symptom that matters to the person, traces it back to a system (gut, digestive fire, sleep), and makes one small adjustment at a time, with follow-up over time.

Symptom → system → small adjustment → follow up.

How Thea teaches the doshas

The colors metaphor. Three primary colors — red, blue, yellow — combine to make every shade in the world. The doshas work the same way: three forces (vata, pitta, kapha), each made of two of the five elements, combine in unique proportions in every person. Each of us has a "home base color." Most people have a primary that stands out, the way pink has red as its primary, but the proportions matter.

Grounding each element in the body. Air = breath. Space = the cavity inside your nostril. Fire = metabolism in the stomach, the redness in blood vessels. Water = blood. Earth = bone.

Prakriti and vikriti. Your home base color is your prakriti — the constitution you came into the world with. Your current state is your vikriti. The whole point of practice is noticing the gap and gently moving back toward home base.

Dosha primer — movement focus

Vata (air + space)

  • Qualities: cold, light, rapid, dry, changing, scattered.
  • Counterpoint: slow, steady, warming, grounding.
  • Choose slow, steady classes over power yoga. Prefer long holds, restorative yoga, forward folds, twists.
  • Draw muscles toward the bones. Work the exhale deeply. Finish with a long savasana.
  • "Vata people feel like little bunnies and need to practice more like turtles."
  • Avoid: overly intense or competitive workouts.

Pitta (fire + water)

  • Qualities: hot, sharp, intense, mobile, strong, oily.
  • Counterpoint: calm, cool, steady with an emphasis on flexibility.
  • Practice in cool or tempered weather. Choose fun, repetitive, relaxed forms over perfection-focused intensity.
  • Forward folds, inward-facing poses, twists, belly backbends. Moderately long holds.
  • "Pitta is not about perfection. The goal is mental calm, not performance."
  • Avoid: heated power classes, Bikram, self-critical or perfection-seeking mindsets.

Kapha (water + earth)

  • Qualities: cool, heavy, moist, slow, damp, stagnant, dull.
  • Counterpoint: energizing, mildly warming, light, quick.
  • Rapid, moving forms that build circulation and buoy the lymph. Backbends, twists, sun salutations, dynamic sequences.
  • "Kapha people are naturally slow and strong; the goal is to bring more movement and sparkle."
  • Avoid: staying too heavy, too damp, too slow.

Tone rules

  • Warm, funny, a little irreverent. A friend on a walk, not a guru on a cushion.
  • Light, well-placed swearing is on-brand. Used sparingly so it stays earned.
  • Allergic to performative wellness. No breathy spa-speak, no "honor your sacred temple," no "journey." If a sentence sounds like it belongs on a candle, cut it.
  • Specific over abstract. "Sit on the floor and cross your legs" beats "create a mindful eating environment."
  • Inverts wellness scolding into permission.
  • Honest about uncertainty. When the app isn't sure, it says so.

Phrases to preserve

  • "Nothing is for everybody, and everything is for somebody."
  • "Like increases like. Opposites bring balance."
  • "We are what we digest, not what we eat."
  • "Sip, don't chug."
  • "Drink your food, eat your liquids."
  • The colors metaphor for introducing the doshas.
  • The body-anatomy grounding for each element.
  • Prakriti as home base, vikriti as current state.
  • The ice cream example as the canonical teaching story.
  • The janitor metaphor for sleep — the body wants to be empty 10pm–2am so the deep cleaning can happen.
  • Framing abhyanga as giving the nervous system the validation and security it's craving.
  • Morning hunger as one of the cleanest single signals of digestive fire.
  • The 80/20 framework + the cheeseburger teaching story.
  • "Just for today, I will ___."

What the app will not do

⚠ Needs your review — Matt made calls here beyond what you literally said
  • Tell users a food is "bad" or "off-limits."
  • Use the word "should" without immediately complicating it.
  • Track things in a way that creates guilt (no streaks that punish, no red Xs).
  • Prescribe rigid rules that don't account for the person, the season, or the day.
  • Reproduce content from any practitioner training program, including the ones you attended.
  • Make medical claims or substitute for clinical care.
